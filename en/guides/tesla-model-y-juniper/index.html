Tesla Model Y "Juniper": what's new in the facelift?

The updated Model Y, known by the codename Juniper, is a thorough refinement of the car that was Denmark's best-selling vehicle in 2023 and 2024. Here's a level-headed look at what actually changed, and what to keep an eye on.

Last updated: June 2026

Tesla doesn't call it "Juniper" in its sales material, but the name has stuck as the easy way to tell the facelifted Model Y apart from the earlier version. It isn't an all-new car, but a careful update that tackles several of the things owners used to complain about: cabin noise, a slightly firm ride, and an ageing design. If you buy a new Model Y today, this is the version you get.

Exterior design

The lighting is the most eye-catching change. The front gets a slim light bar running across the nose, and the headlights are slimmer and more understated. At the back sits a full-width light bar that casts its light indirectly, with the Tesla logo placed centrally on the tailgate instead of the previous lettering. The body's basic shape is the same, but the details give the car a newer, more cohesive look.

Interior

The cabin is where the update is felt most day to day. Tesla has added a small touchscreen between the front seats so rear passengers can control climate and entertainment. The seats are improved and, depending on variant and market, can be had with ventilation. There's new ambient lighting along the doors and dash, and, not least, Tesla has put real work into sound insulation, so there's noticeably less road and wind noise than before. The central screen and the control philosophy are fundamentally the same, with everything still run from the large display.

Ride comfort and suspension

One of the most common criticisms of the earlier Model Y was a slightly firm ride. With the facelift, Tesla has reworked the geometry and fitted new dampers that filter out bumps better. The result is a softer, more settled ride without the car losing its composure through corners. For many families, that kind of everyday comfort is exactly what makes the difference.

Range and charging

Efficiency is slightly improved thanks to better aerodynamics and the new suspension, and the most efficient Long Range RWD variants remain among the strongest in the class for range. The specific WLTP figures depend on variant, wheels and market and change over time, so always confirm the current numbers for your configuration. You'll find an overview of the variants in the Model Y guide. The car still charges quickly on the Supercharger network, around 250 kW on supported variants, and most owners charge at home day to day.

What to keep an eye on

  • There's no 360-degree parking camera, which several rivals in the price range offer.
  • The retractable door handles can, on rare occasions, freeze or play up in winter, see winter preparation.
  • The four-year or 80,000 km warranty is shorter than the five years or 150,000 km some European rivals now offer, see Tesla warranty.

Is it the right car for you?

If you're buying new, the choice is simple, because the facelifted Model Y is what's on sale today, and it's noticeably more refined than its predecessor. If you're considering a used Model Y, it can be worth seeking out the newer version for the comfort and the lower noise, but a well-kept earlier model is still a good, spacious EV. If you're choosing between models, compare it with the Model 3 and work through the full cost of ownership.
